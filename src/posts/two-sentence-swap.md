Most advice about visibility assumes the hard part is knowing what to say. For a lot of people, that is not the hard part. The hard part is the feeling that arrives the second you go to say it, the small flinch of *who am I to take up space*. If that is you, you do not have a content problem. You have a performing problem, and the usual fix, post more and be more confident, is the exact thing you are trying to avoid.

So do not perform. Swap it.

## The swap

Find one person who has genuinely liked your work. A customer, a reader, a client, a friend who once said the thing that stuck with you. Ask them for two sentences: what they came to you for, and what changed. That is it. Two sentences.

Then post *their* words, credited to them, as the caption. You are still in the picture. You made the thing. But you are not the one doing the selling, and that turns the volume all the way down on the flinch.

Here is why it works, and it is not a trick.

## Why it lands harder than anything you would write

When you describe your own work, people hear a claim. When someone else describes it, people hear evidence. The sentence is doing completely different work depending on whose mouth it comes from, even if the words are almost identical.

There is a well-documented pattern behind this: people who promote themselves tend to be liked less and judged no more capable, because the audience quietly discounts anything a person says about themselves. A recommendation routes around that discount entirely. You are not asking to be trusted. You are showing that you already are.

## How to actually do it this week

1. **Pick one person.** Not the biggest name. The most specific one, the one who can say what actually changed.
2. **Ask small.** "Would you mind writing two sentences, what you came to me for and what changed? No pressure on length." Small asks get said yes to.
3. **Use their words as the caption.** Their name, their sentences, your work in the frame. Add nothing that sounds like a pitch.
4. **Do it again next month.** One quiet, credited post a month builds a wall of proof over a year, and you never once had to describe yourself.

That is the whole method. You are not hiding. You are letting the work talk, which was always the plan. You are just making sure the sentence comes from the person best placed to say it, which is never you.
